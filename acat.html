ACAT 2.0 explores how eye-tracking can be used to give MND patients a faster way to communicate.

Assistive Context-Aware Toolkit (ACAT) is an open source platform enabling people with motor neuron diseases to have full access to the capabilities of their computers through very constrained interfaces. In late 2019, Fjord and Intel Labs partnered to design and develop a new, eye-tracking enabled interface for ACAT, an assistive technology system originally developed for Stephen Hawking.

We designed and implemented a novel spherical keyboard design and a set of useful new features such as alarms, phrasebooks and shortcuts based on our embedded research approach. We worked with a cross-disciplinary design, including linguistic experts, AI researchers and development teams spread across the US and UK. I was responsible for implementing the new UI/UX designs into the legacy codebase and co-facilitating research, testing and ideation sessions throughout the project.

We tested ACAT 2.0 with Peter Scott-Morgan, an English-American roboticist leveraging new technology in order to THRIVE with MND. Peter is now using ACAT 2.0 as his primary means of communication.
